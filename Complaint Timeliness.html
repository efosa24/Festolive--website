How we improved complaint timeliness from 89% to 98%

A practical look at how better metrics, focused dashboards, and data-driven process changes helped a global organization reduce late complaints and improve oversight.

The starting point: many reports, no single story

When we first engaged with this complaint team, timeliness was already being tracked — but in different spreadsheets, regional reports, and ad-hoc PowerPoints. Leaders saw numbers like “89% on-time”, but no one could easily answer:

  • Which complaints are actually late right now?
  • Where are we losing time in the process (triage, investigation, closure)?
  • Which products, regions, or owner groups drive most lateness?

The result: firefighting, delayed escalations, and regular surprises close to compliance deadlines.

Our goal wasn’t just to “improve the percentage.” It was to make lateness visible early, at the right level (global, regional, owner group), so teams could intervene before deadlines were missed.

Step 1 – Define a clear timeliness logic everyone trusts

The first step was to agree on a single, simple definition of complaint timeliness that could be applied consistently across regions.

Key decisions we aligned with stakeholders

  • Clock start: Which date determines when the “timer” begins (e.g., awareness date vs. system entry date).
  • On-time vs late rule: For example, complaints closed within ≤ 45 days counted as on-time; anything above that threshold was late.
  • Scope of analysis: Exclude test cases, withdrawn complaints, or specific “no action” categories so the metric focused on meaningful workload.

We then translated this logic into a set of repeatable calculations using SQL and/or Python so that every dashboard would always show the same view of timeliness — no matter who pulled the report.

Step 2 – Build a dynamic view of on-time vs late

With the metric defined, the next move was to build a visual layer that made performance obvious at a glance.

Core design patterns we used

  • A global doughnut chart showing on-time vs late rates with color thresholds (green, yellow, red) based on target levels (e.g., 98%).
  • Regional breakdowns so leaders could see which areas were pulling the overall metric up or down.
  • Drill-down by owner group and product, so teams could identify where lateness was concentrated.

Instead of scrolling through rows in Excel, managers could immediately answer: “Where are we late, and who needs help?”

Step 3 – Add process analytics: where is time being lost?

Improving the percentage required more than just measuring it. We needed to see where time was leaking within the complaint lifecycle.

  • 1
    Stage-level timing
    We calculated days spent in key stages (e.g., triage, investigation, closure) for each complaint, using process step start/end dates.
  • 2
    Thresholds by risk level
    Higher-risk cases (serious events, priority products) were given tighter internal thresholds so they appeared earlier on the radar.
  • 3
    Exception-focused views
    We built views focused only on cases approaching or exceeding the threshold so reviewers didn’t have to scan thousands of records.
Once everyone could see “where the days were going,” conversations shifted from blaming individuals to adjusting workloads, redistributing cases, and refining triage rules.

Step 4 – Operationalize: alerts, routines, and ownership

A dashboard alone doesn’t move metrics. The improvement from 89% to 98% came when we paired analytics with clear routines and ownership.

What changed in day-to-day work

  • Weekly timeliness review: Each region had a short, focused review using the same dashboard, highlighting late and at-risk cases.
  • Owner-group accountability: Late cases were grouped by owner team, making bottlenecks visible and solvable.
  • Early-warning alerts: Optional alerts (e.g., email, Teams) for cases hitting internal “warning” thresholds.

Over a few months, teams became proactive — instead of discovering late cases at the end of the month, they were addressed days or weeks earlier.

Step 5 – Iterate, refine, and scale

As timeliness improved, leaders wanted to extend the approach to:

  • Specific product families and priority markets.
  • Complaint types (e.g., safety vs. non-safety issues).
  • Additional metrics like aging backlog and aging open cases.

Because the underlying data model and definitions were already set, scaling the analytics to these new questions became much faster — often a matter of extending filters, segments, or visual slices.

What this means for your organization

Improving timeliness isn’t magic — but it does require clarity across data, metrics, and process. The key ingredients in this case were:

  • A trusted definition of “on-time” vs “late.”
  • Dashboards built around exceptions and action, not just charts.
  • Stage-level analytics to see where the time is lost.
  • Simple, repeatable review routines that teams actually use.

If you’re currently seeing numbers like “89% on-time” and want to move closer to “98%” — the starting point is often the same: audit your definitions, centralize the data, and build one clear view that your teams can rally around.
